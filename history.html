Noteworthy Places in Beijing's History

1573

Forbidden City

At the beginning of the 15th century AD, the third Ming Emperor, Yung-lo, took control of the imperial city used during the Mongol Yuan Dynasty. He commanded that the Yuan palaces be razed in anticipation of the construction of the Forbidden Palace. The 9000 room palace took 15 years to be constructed and used materials from sites throughout the country. The Forbidden Palace had a symmetrical layout with buildings gilded with gold roofs and green, red, and yellow facings. From 1421 to 1911, the site was home to all of China’s emperors. The emperor would oversee processions for festivals and military victories from the front gate, Wumen Gate. With the resignation of the last emperor of China, Puyi, in 1912, the Forbidden City was handed over to Republican Authorities. With an agreement signed by the emperor and the new government, Puyi and his family was allowed to stay in the inner court, while the republican authorities took control of the outer court. Today, the Forbidden City is one of the largest museums in China and is a memento of China’s imperial past.

Temple of Heaven

Originally built in 1420, the Temple of Heaven has become the largest (larger than the Forbidden City) and most representative ancient sacrificial building in China. Since its original construction, the site has been expanded and reconstructed two other times, once in the Ming dynasty and again in the Qing dynasty. Prior to its opening to the public in 1988 and during the time of the emperors, the temple would be visited by the emperor once a year. The emperor would visit during the winter solstice to worship Heaven and pray for good harvest. The wall that encloses the site represents heaen on the northern side (with a semicircle configuration) and earth on the southern side (with a square configuration). The site was later labeled as World Cultural Heritage by UNESCO.

1700

Summer Palace

The Summer Palace, also known as Yiheyuan in Chinese. As it is known today, the Summer Palace is 2.9 square kilometers with lakes, gardens, and palaces. The two largest geographical features are Kunming Lake and Longevity Hill. The beginnings of the Summer Palace can be traced to the Jin dynasty, 1153 AD, with the fourth ruler Wanyan Liang. Liang ordered the construction of a palace in the northwestern area of Beijing. It wasn’t until 1271 and 1272 that the city engineer started work on a water project to transport water from Shenshan Spring to the Western Lake, which would later become Kunming Lake. In 1494, the Hongzhi Emperor oversaw the construction of Yuanjing Temple on Jar Hill, the location of the future Longevity Hill. In the years that followed the temple fell into disrepair until the Zhengde Emperor chose to build a palace in the same location. In 1749 the lake was expanded and the Summer Palace was built in 1750, founded on the legend in Chinese mythology about three divine mountains. The palace was destroyed in 1860 and later restored in 1886. After the resignation of Puyi, the Summer Palace was opened to the public in 1914. It wasn’t until 1949 that the Palace served a political purpose, to house the Central Party School of the Communist Party of China. These days, the site is a UNESCO Heritage Site and one of the top historic sites in Beijing.

Beihai Park

Beihai Park has been named one of the oldest and most authentically preserved imperial gardens. Built in the 11th century, the Park is home to a large lake (literally translated as the "Northern Sea"). At the center of the lake is Jade Flower Island and the site is also home to an imperial garden. Although most buildings (including the many temples) were constructed during the Qing Dynasty, Beihai has existed since the Liao dynasties. The site was built according to traditional Chinese legend that speaks to a body of water accompanied by three mountains.

Jingshan Park

Once an private imperial park, the Jingshan Park lies on the central axis of Beijing, to the north of the Forbidden City. The focal point is the manmade hill called "Jing-Shan," literally meaninng "Prospect Hill." The entire hill was built during the Yongle Era of the Ming Dynasty, with soil excavated from the project forming the moats of the Imperial Palace. At the peak, one can have a comprehensive view of the Forbidden City. The park is also known by the public as the place where the last emperor of the Ming Dynasty hanged himself.

1861

Yonghe Temple

The construction of Yonghe Temple (also known as Yonghe Lamasery and Lama Temple) began in 1694 and was the official residence for court eunuchs. In 1722, half of the building was converted into a monastery for monks and since then has been renowned as the most famous Tibetan Buddhist temple ouide of Tibet. The temple became a national monument in 1949 and closed for 32 years afterward. The site survived the Cultural Revolution, in part due to the efforts of Premier Zhou Enlai. In 1981 the site was reopened. The Temple is comprised of five main halls: Hall of the Heavenly Kings, Hall of Harmony and Peace, Hall of Everlasting Protection, Hall of the Wheel of the Law, and the Pavilion of Ten Thousand Happinesses.

Qianmen Gate

The Qianmen Gate is located on the central axis of Beijing, south to the Tian'anmen Square. Its original name is Zhengyang Gate, which was first build in 1419 during the Ming Dynasty. As a gate in Beijing's historic city wall, it once served as the southern entrance into the Inner City. Historically, it consisted of a gatehouse and an archery tower, connected by side walls and side gates. Today, however, only the gatehouse and archery tower remain, surviving the domolition of city walls during the contruction of the Beijing Subway.

1900

Chongwenmen Outer Street

In the east part of the street is a famous pharmacy called Qianzhitang. The brand was born 1745; and in 1881, Wu Aiting bought the store and hired Wang Zifeng to be the manager. Wang was an expert in both medicine and management. With Wang's effort, the pharmacy became prosperous. Wang and Wu had many conflicts. Finally, Wang left to start a new pharmacy in the north of Qianzhitang that became famous for its valuable medicines. Wang and Wu, along with Tongrentang and Heniantan, became the most famous pharmacies in Beijing.

Di'an Gate Outer Street

Di'an Gate is the north gate of the Imperial City. The 77th street is home to the oldest temple for fire, named Huode Zhenjun. The temple was built in the Tang Dynasty and underwent several renovations in its history. In order to avoid the busy duck, the gate of the temple faced east. Empress Dowager Cixi paid a visit to the temple to pray for the safety of the city in the face of conflagration. On the opposite of the temple there was a shop selling sausage. Cixi smelt the sausage and asked the manager to send her some. She went crazy over it and decreed that the shop stay open forever.

1938

Da Xiangfeng Hutong

This Hutong was very influential in the development of Chinese modern literature. There were many writers living here, such as Ma Feng and Ding Ling. It was also the home of Cau Xueqin, the writer of Dream of Red Mansions.

Hufang Road

This road is famous for a house that keeps tigers. Where the north side of the street was left for tigers, Zeng Guofan lived on the 3rd street and the 5th street was used as a guild hall.

1952

Qianmen Street

Qianmen was one of the nine gates of Beijing's inner city. Its official name is Zhengyang Gate and was built in 1420 AD, during the Ming Dynasty. The archery tower of Qianmen Gate is treated as a symbol of Beijing. In the year 1900 AD, the emperor and empress dowager Cixi escaped from the inner city under the attack of the Eight-Power Allied Forces. When the emperor came back (when?) Zhengyang Gate was distroyed . In order to conform with the etiquette of Qing Dynasty, a temperary Zhnegyang Gate was built using silk and wood. The gate was then rebuilt several times in order to improve traffic. The Qianmen Railway station has been the largest station in Beijing until 1949. (when was the railway station constructed?)

West Chang'an Street

This street is famous for the Ming Dynasty court. Officials who rode a horse here must get off immediately to show their owe to the law.

1980

Zhushikou West Street

During the Ming Dynasty, this street was famous for trading pigs. During the Qing Dynasty, the pig market was replaced with beautiful theaters.

Dashila Street

This area is a famous commercial center. Historically, by the end of the Ming Dynasty, there were approximately 80 shops here.

2005

National Stadium

The Chinese National Stadium, which is better known as the "Bird Nest," was built for the 2008 Beijing Olympics Games. It is located in the north side of the city, on the central axis of Beijing. The circular shape of the stadium represents "heaven," while the adjacent Nationa Aquatics Center (known as "Water Cube") in a square form representing "earth." Together, they reflect the traditional cuture of Chinese, emphasizing the harmony of the heaven and the earth. It is one of the largest stadiums in the world, with a gross floor area of 254,600 square meter and seating capacity of 91,000, including 11,000 temporary seats.

Fifth Ring Road

Beijing's 5th Ring Road is a ring road encircling the city about 10 kilometres (6.2 mi) away from the city centre. It takes the form of an expressway and is 98 kilometres (61 mi) in length. First portion of the ring road opened in 2001. This shows the pace of which modern Beijing had expanded in the period of 20-30 years.
